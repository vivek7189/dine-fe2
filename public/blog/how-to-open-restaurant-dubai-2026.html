2. Trade License: DET Mainland vs Free Zone Options

Every restaurant in Dubai needs a trade license before it can legally operate. You have two main paths: a mainland license through the Department of Economy and Tourism (DET), or a free zone license. The choice fundamentally affects where you can operate, who you can serve, and how much you pay.

Mainland License (DET) — Recommended for Restaurants

  • Cost: AED 15,000-25,000 (initial registration + activity fees)
  • Key advantage: You can open your restaurant anywhere in Dubai and serve any customer — no geographic restrictions
  • Ownership: 100% foreign ownership is permitted since the 2020 Commercial Companies Law amendment
  • Visa quota: Based on office/restaurant space — typically 1 visa per 30 sqft of space
  • Renewal: Annual renewal, approximately AED 10,000-15,000

Free Zone Options

  • DMCC Free Zone: AED 25,000-50,000. Best for food trading companies, not ideal for dine-in restaurants as operations are limited to DMCC areas
  • DAFZA (Dubai Airport Free Zone): AED 20,000-40,000. Suitable for catering companies serving the airport and surrounding areas
  • IFZA / Meydan Free Zone: AED 12,000-20,000. Lower cost but very limited food service options

Bottom line: If you are opening a restaurant where customers walk in and eat, get a mainland DET license. Free zones are designed for trading, manufacturing, and logistics — not customer-facing food service. The small savings on license fees are not worth the operational restrictions.

Required Documents for DET Trade License

  • Passport copies of all shareholders (with valid UAE visa or entry stamp)
  • Emirates ID (if already a UAE resident)
  • No Objection Certificate (NOC) from current sponsor (if switching from employment to business visa)
  • Business plan or feasibility study
  • Tenancy contract (Ejari) for the restaurant premises
  • Dubai Municipality initial approval
  • Civil Defence approval (fire safety)

Timeline: 2-4 weeks from document submission to license issuance, assuming all paperwork is in order. Using a business setup consultant (AED 3,000-8,000) can speed this up and avoid common mistakes that cause delays.

3. Dubai Municipality Food Safety Permit

Your trade license alone does not permit you to serve food. You need a separate food safety permit from Dubai Municipality (for mainland restaurants) or Trakhees (for restaurants in free zone areas like JAFZA, Palm Jumeirah, and Dubai World). This is arguably the most critical approval in the entire process — without it, you cannot open your doors.

The Approval Process

  1. Initial application: Submit floor plans, kitchen layout, ventilation details, and proposed menu to Dubai Municipality's Food Safety Department (Baladia)
  2. Plan review: Municipal engineers review your kitchen design for compliance with food safety standards (drainage, ventilation, hand-washing stations, storage separation). This takes 1-2 weeks
  3. Fit-out inspection: Once your kitchen is built, a Dubai Municipality inspector visits to verify compliance with the approved plans. You must pass before receiving a food permit
  4. Food handler medical fitness: Every employee who handles food must pass a medical fitness test (blood test, chest X-ray) at a DHA-approved medical centre. Cost: AED 200-350 per person
  5. Food Safety Certification: At least one staff member (typically the head chef or kitchen manager) must hold a valid food safety certificate from CIEH, ServSafe, or an equivalent accredited body

The Grading System

Dubai Municipality rates all food establishments on an A/B/C system. Your grade is publicly displayed at the entrance. Grade A means excellent compliance, Grade B is satisfactory, and Grade C requires improvement within a specific timeframe or faces closure. Inspections are unannounced and can happen at any time. Maintaining digital food safety records through your POS system speeds up inspections and demonstrates professionalism to inspectors.

Key Kitchen Requirements

  • Separate preparation areas for raw and cooked foods
  • Dedicated hand-washing sinks (not shared with dish-washing)
  • Adequate cold storage with temperature monitoring
  • Pest control contract with a municipality-approved provider
  • Grease trap installation (mandatory for all commercial kitchens)
  • HACCP food safety management system documentation

Renewal: Food permits must be renewed annually. During renewal, expect another inspection. Restaurants that maintain consistent digital records and temperature logs have significantly easier renewals.

Per Employee Visa Cost Breakdown

Visa Categories for Restaurant Staff

  • Employment visa: The standard option for all restaurant employees — chefs, waiters, cleaners, cashiers, managers. Valid for 2-3 years, renewable
  • Partner visa: For business partners or investors who hold shares in the restaurant company. Allows the holder to work in the business and sponsor additional staff
  • Golden visa: Available to investors who invest AED 2 million or more. Provides 10-year residency, which is useful for restaurant group owners

Sponsorship Responsibilities

As a restaurant owner, you are the visa sponsor for all your employees. This means you are legally responsible for their health insurance, end-of-service gratuity (21 days salary per year for the first 5 years, 30 days per year after that), and repatriation costs if they leave the country. Budget an additional AED 2,000-3,000 per employee per year for renewals and insurance. Factor this into your ongoing operational costs — it adds up quickly with 10-15 staff members.

7. VAT 5% Setup & Compliance

VAT Registration: Mandatory for Almost Every Restaurant

If your restaurant's taxable supplies exceed AED 375,000 per year (approximately AED 31,250/month), VAT registration is mandatory. Most restaurants in Dubai exceed this threshold within their first 2-3 months of operation. Voluntary registration is available even below the threshold if you want to reclaim input VAT on your startup costs.

  • Registration: Apply through the Federal Tax Authority (FTA) portal. You will receive a Tax Registration Number (TRN) within 2-4 weeks
  • TRN on invoices: Your TRN must appear on every customer receipt and invoice. Your POS system should print this automatically
  • Quarterly filing: VAT returns are filed quarterly through the FTA portal. Deadlines are strict — late filing penalties start at AED 1,000 and increase to AED 2,500 for repeat offences
  • Record keeping: You must maintain VAT records for a minimum of 5 years. Digital records through your POS system are the easiest way to comply
  • Input VAT recovery: You can reclaim 5% VAT paid on business expenses (equipment, ingredients, rent) against the VAT you collect from customers
  • Penalties: Non-compliance penalties range from AED 2,500 for late registration to AED 50,000 for repeated violations or fraudulent reporting

The smartest move is to use a VAT-compliant POS system from day one. Retroactively adding VAT compliance to manual records or a non-compliant system is painful, error-prone, and expensive (accountant fees of AED 5,000-15,000 to sort out). 9. Seasonal Business Planning: Ramadan, Summer & Tourist Season

Dubai's restaurant calendar has dramatic seasonal swings that catch unprepared owners off guard. Understanding these patterns and planning for them is the difference between surviving your first year and thriving beyond it.

The Four Seasons of Dubai Dining

Tourist Season (October - March): Peak Revenue

This is when Dubai comes alive. Temperatures drop to comfortable levels, tourists flood in for winter sun, and outdoor dining becomes possible. Expect 30-50% higher revenue compared to summer months. Restaurants in tourist-heavy areas like JBR, Downtown, and Dubai Marina see the biggest spikes. Staff up, stock up, and maximize your seating capacity. This is when you build the financial cushion that carries you through summer.

Ramadan (Varies: March-April in 2026): Transform Your Model

During Ramadan, restaurants cannot serve food during daylight hours (to non-fasting individuals without proper screening). However, the iftar (sunset meal) market is enormous. Hotels and restaurants compete for iftar buffet bookings at AED 150-500 per person. Suhoor (pre-dawn meal) is another revenue opportunity from 10 PM to 3 AM. If you plan Ramadan well, this month can actually exceed normal monthly revenue.

Summer (June - August): The Survival Period

Temperatures exceed 45 degrees Celsius and a significant portion of Dubai's expatriate population travels abroad. Dine-in traffic drops 30-50% in many areas. However, delivery orders spike as people stay home in air conditioning. This is when a strong delivery presence on Talabat and Deliveroo becomes critical. Reduce dine-in staff hours, push delivery promotions, and use this time for menu updates and staff training.

National Day & New Year (December): Revenue Spikes

UAE National Day (December 2) and New Year's Eve are two of the biggest dining nights of the year. Restaurants near Burj Khalifa, JBR, and the Palm report 200-300% normal revenue on New Year's Eve alone. Plan special menus, take reservations early, and ensure your POS and kitchen can handle the volume.

Cash Flow Planning by Season

  • October-March: Build reserves. Save 15-20% of revenue for the summer slowdown
  • Ramadan: Invest in iftar/suhoor marketing 4-6 weeks before. Pre-sell iftar packages
  • June-August: Cut variable costs, focus on delivery, offer summer promotions to locals who stayed
  • December: Plan special events and fixed-price menus 8 weeks in advance

10. Top 10 Mistakes Expat Restaurant Owners Make in Dubai

After speaking with dozens of restaurant owners across Dubai who have succeeded (and failed), these are the most common and costly mistakes that new entrants make. Avoid these, and you dramatically improve your odds of surviving past year one.

  1. Underestimating rent deposits. Most landlords demand 3-6 months upfront as a security deposit. On a AED 300/sqft location, that is AED 75,000-150,000 for a 500 sqft space — locked up before you even start building. Budget for this separately from your fit-out costs.
  2. Not budgeting for staff visas. The AED 5,000-8,000 per employee visa cost shocks first-time owners. With 10 staff, that is AED 50,000-80,000 in visa costs alone before anyone starts working. Plus ongoing costs for renewals, insurance, and end-of-service benefits.
  3. Choosing the wrong location based on ego, not data. Opening in DIFC or Downtown Dubai sounds prestigious, but AED 400-600/sqft rent requires massive revenue to sustain. Many successful restaurants thrive in Karama, JLT, or Business Bay at half the rent, serving a loyal local clientele.
  4. Ignoring delivery platforms. Some owners view Talabat's 30-35% commission as too expensive and refuse to list. This is a mistake. Delivery platforms provide visibility and volume that is nearly impossible to replicate on your own. The commission is essentially a marketing cost. Start with all platforms, then optimize based on which ones deliver the best ROI.
  5. Poor Arabic/English menu design. A menu that only works in English alienates Arabic-speaking customers. A poorly translated Arabic menu is even worse. Invest in proper bilingual menu design from day one. Use a digital menu system that supports both languages natively.
  6. Not understanding Ramadan's impact. First-time owners who do not plan for Ramadan lose money during what could be a highly profitable period. Study the iftar buffet market, adjust operating hours, and create special Ramadan offerings at least 6 weeks before the holy month begins.
  7. Over-investing in interior fit-out. Spending AED 500,000+ on interiors for a 50-seat restaurant is a common trap. Customers care about food quality and service first, ambiance second. Cap your fit-out at 25-30% of total startup budget and invest the rest in kitchen equipment, technology, and working capital.
  8. Not getting a VAT-compliant POS from day one. Starting with manual billing or a non-compliant system and "planning to upgrade later" leads to messy records, potential FTA penalties, and expensive retroactive fixes. A VAT-compliant POS system costs AED 75-150/month — far less than the AED 5,000-15,000 accountant bill to sort out non-compliant records.
  9. Hiring too many staff too early. Dubai's visa system means every hire is a significant financial commitment. Start lean with technology to handle order-taking (QR ordering), kitchen management (KDS), and delivery (integrated POS). Add staff only when demand consistently exceeds your current capacity.
  10. No financial runway for the first 6-12 months. Most new restaurants in Dubai take 6-12 months to reach break-even. Without adequate working capital to cover rent, salaries, and inventory during this ramp-up period, many owners are forced to close before they ever get the chance to succeed. Budget at least 6 months of operating costs beyond your startup investment.
