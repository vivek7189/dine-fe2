1. VAT Basics for UAE Restaurants

The UAE introduced Value Added Tax on January 1, 2018, applying a standard rate of 5% to the supply of most goods and services. For restaurants, this means 5% VAT is applied to all food and beverages served — whether dine-in, takeaway, or delivery. There is no reduced rate for food in the UAE, unlike many European countries that offer lower VAT rates on essential food items.

The 5% standard rate applies uniformly across all restaurant scenarios:

  • Dine-in meals: 5% VAT on the total bill including food, beverages, and any mandatory service charges
  • Takeaway orders: 5% VAT (no reduced rate, unlike the UK where takeaway food can be zero-rated)
  • Delivery orders: 5% VAT on food items plus the delivery charge
  • Catering services: 5% VAT on the full catering amount
  • Exported goods: Zero-rated (0%), though this is rare for restaurants

The Federal Tax Authority (FTA) is the government body responsible for administering and enforcing VAT in the UAE. All VAT-registered businesses must file returns, maintain records, and issue compliant invoices. The FTA conducts audits and has the authority to impose significant penalties for non-compliance — making it critical that your restaurant's billing system handles VAT correctly from day one.

2. VAT Registration Requirements

Registration Thresholds

  • Mandatory registration: Annual taxable supplies exceed AED 375,000 (approximately AED 31,250/month)
  • Voluntary registration: Annual taxable supplies exceed AED 187,500
  • Reality check: Most restaurants must register — AED 375,000/year is roughly equivalent to a small cafe with 3 tables turning twice daily

Registration Process

  • Where: Online via the FTA e-Services portal (eservices.tax.gov.ae)
  • Required documents: Trade license, passport copies of owners/partners, Emirates ID, bank account details, financial records (last 12 months)
  • Processing time: 5-20 business days
  • TRN (Tax Registration Number): A unique 15-digit number assigned upon approval — this must appear on every invoice you issue

Important: You must register within 30 days of exceeding the mandatory threshold. Late registration carries a penalty of AED 20,000.

4. How VAT Applies to Restaurant Scenarios

VAT treatment varies depending on the type of sale. Here is how 5% VAT applies to every common restaurant scenario in the UAE.

Dine-In

5% VAT applies to the total bill, including food, beverages, and any mandatory service charge. If your restaurant adds a 10% service charge to every bill, VAT is calculated on the combined amount (food + service charge). For example: AED 200 food + AED 20 service charge = AED 220 taxable amount, with AED 11 VAT, for a total of AED 231.

Takeaway

5% VAT applies to all takeaway orders. Unlike the UK, where certain cold takeaway food is zero-rated, the UAE applies the standard 5% rate to all food regardless of whether it is consumed on or off premises. There is no reduced rate for takeaway in the UAE.

Delivery via Own Fleet

5% VAT applies to both the food amount and the delivery charge. If you charge AED 150 for food and AED 10 for delivery, VAT is 5% on AED 160 = AED 8. The total billed to the customer is AED 168.

Delivery via Talabat/Deliveroo

5% VAT applies to the food amount billed to the customer. The delivery platform's commission and delivery charges to the restaurant are subject to separate VAT treatment — you will receive a VAT invoice from Talabat or Deliveroo for their services, which counts as your input tax (reclaimable).

Catering Orders

5% VAT applies to the full catering amount, including food preparation, service staff, equipment rental, and any other charges bundled into the catering contract. Catering for events is one of the few scenarios where invoices regularly exceed AED 10,000, requiring full tax invoice format with customer details.

Staff Meals

Generally, no VAT applies to staff meals consumed on premises as part of employment benefits. These are treated as internal consumption, not a taxable supply. However, if you operate a staff canteen that charges employees for meals, those charges would be subject to VAT.

5. VAT on Tips, Service Charges & Discounts

This is where most restaurants get it wrong. The VAT treatment of tips, service charges, and discounts is nuanced, and errors here are among the top reasons for FTA audit findings.

Service Charges

Subject to VAT. If your restaurant adds a mandatory service charge (e.g., 10%) to every bill, this amount is considered part of the consideration for the supply. VAT at 5% must be calculated on the combined total of food/beverages plus the service charge. Your POS must add the service charge first, then calculate VAT on the resulting amount.

Voluntary Tips

Not subject to VAT. Tips left voluntarily by customers — whether cash on the table, added to a card payment, or entered on a POS prompt — are not consideration for a supply. They are voluntary payments and therefore fall outside the scope of VAT. Your POS must NOT include tip amounts when calculating VAT.

Discounts at Time of Sale

Reduce the taxable amount. If you offer a 20% discount on a AED 100 meal, VAT is calculated on AED 80 (the discounted price), not AED 100. This means the VAT amount is AED 4, and the total bill is AED 84. Your POS must apply discounts before calculating VAT, not after.

Loyalty Points and Rewards

Complex — seek tax advisor input. The VAT treatment of loyalty points depends on whether they are treated as a discount at the time of redemption or as a separate supply. If a customer redeems 500 points for a free dessert, the VAT treatment depends on your loyalty program structure. Consult your tax advisor for your specific program design.

Promotional Offers (Buy One Get One Free)

VAT on the amount actually charged. If a customer buys one main course at AED 80 and gets a second one free, VAT is calculated on AED 80 only. The free item is not a separate supply — it is part of the single transaction at the stated price.

6. Filing VAT Returns

VAT Return Filing Requirements

  • Filing frequency: Usually quarterly (some businesses monthly if annual revenue exceeds AED 150 million)
  • Deadline: 28th day after the end of the tax period (e.g., Q1 Jan-Mar return due by April 28)
  • Filing method: Online via the FTA e-Services portal
  • Payment deadline: Same as filing deadline — payment must be made by the 28th day
  • Record retention: Keep all records for a minimum of 5 years

What your VAT return includes: Total value of supplies and expenses, total output VAT (collected from customers), total input VAT (paid to suppliers), and the net VAT payable or refundable. Your POS should generate summary reports that map directly to these fields, making filing a matter of minutes rather than hours of manual calculation.

7. FTA Penalty Schedule for VAT Violations

The Federal Tax Authority does not issue warnings — it issues fines. Understanding the penalty schedule is essential because a single violation can cost more than your entire year's POS subscription. Here is the complete penalty breakdown for restaurant-relevant VAT violations.

Violation Penalty
Late VAT registration AED 20,000
Late filing of VAT return AED 1,000 first time; AED 2,000 repeat (within 24 months)
Late VAT payment 2% immediately + 4% on 7th day + 1% daily (max 300%)
Incorrect VAT return Fixed penalty based on the percentage error
Not issuing tax invoice AED 2,500 per occurrence (first time); AED 5,000 repeat
Not displaying prices inclusive of VAT AED 15,000
Not keeping records for 5 years AED 10,000 first time; AED 50,000 repeat

The late payment penalty deserves special attention. The 1% daily penalty after the 7th day compounds rapidly. If you owe AED 50,000 in VAT and miss the deadline by 30 days, your penalty could reach: AED 1,000 (2% immediate) + AED 2,000 (4% on day 7) + AED 11,500 (1% x 23 remaining days) = AED 14,500 in penalties on top of the original AED 50,000. That is a 29% surcharge for being one month late.

8. How Your POS Should Handle VAT

A VAT-compliant POS system is not a luxury — it is a legal requirement. Here is exactly what your restaurant POS must do to keep you on the right side of the FTA.

Your POS Must Handle All of These

  • Auto-calculate 5% VAT on every transaction without manual input
  • Display VAT as a separate line item on every receipt and invoice
  • Print your TRN on every invoice automatically
  • Handle service charge + VAT correctly — VAT must be calculated on the amount inclusive of service charge
  • Apply VAT correctly on discounts — VAT calculated after the discount is applied, not before
  • Generate VAT summary reports for each filing period (quarterly) with output VAT, input VAT, and net payable
  • Export data for your accountant or tax advisor in standard formats (CSV, PDF)
  • Handle refunds with correct VAT credit — when you refund a customer, the VAT portion must be correctly reversed
  • Display menu prices inclusive of VAT — UAE law requires all displayed prices to include VAT
  • Maintain digital records for 5+ years — cloud-based storage ensures you meet the FTA's record retention requirement

9. Common VAT Mistakes UAE Restaurants Make

These are the errors we see most frequently — and every single one can result in an FTA penalty. Review this list against your current billing setup.

  1. Displaying menu prices exclusive of VAT. UAE law requires all prices shown to customers — on menus, menu boards, websites, and delivery platforms — to be inclusive of VAT. Showing "AED 100 + VAT" instead of "AED 105" is a violation carrying a AED 15,000 penalty.
  2. Wrong TRN on invoices. A single digit typo in your 15-digit TRN renders every invoice invalid. Some restaurants copy their TRN incorrectly during POS setup and do not notice for months — generating hundreds of non-compliant invoices.
  3. Not charging VAT on service charges. The mandatory service charge is part of the taxable supply. If your restaurant charges 10% service charge, VAT must be calculated on the total (food + service charge), not just the food amount.
  4. Calculating VAT on tip amounts. The opposite error: including voluntary tips in the VAT calculation. Tips are not subject to VAT, and charging VAT on tips means you are overcharging VAT and will have discrepancies during audit.
  5. Missing VAT on delivery charges. If you charge customers a delivery fee, that fee is subject to 5% VAT. Many restaurants calculate VAT only on food items and forget to include the delivery charge in the taxable amount.
  6. Not keeping digital records for 5 years. The FTA requires businesses to retain all tax-related records for a minimum of 5 years. Paper receipts fade, get lost, or get damaged. A cloud-based POS ensures your records are preserved automatically.
  7. Filing returns late. The daily 1% penalty after the 7th day compounds fast. Even a short delay can result in penalties that dwarf the actual VAT amount owed.
  8. Not issuing proper tax invoices for B2B catering orders. When you cater for a corporate client, they need a full tax invoice (with their name and TRN if over AED 10,000) to claim input VAT. Failing to provide this means they cannot recover their VAT — and you risk losing the client.
  9. Using a non-compliant POS that does not generate proper VAT invoices. Some POS systems, particularly those designed for other markets, do not support UAE VAT requirements out of the box. If your POS cannot print TRN, calculate VAT correctly, or generate compliant invoices, you are exposed to penalties on every transaction.
